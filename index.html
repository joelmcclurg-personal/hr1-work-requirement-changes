SNAP Work Requirements Changed in 2025

Three major changes affecting millions of SNAP recipients

What Changed?

On July 4, 2025, new federal law (HR1) changed SNAP work requirements for the first time in decades. Three major changes affect millions of people currently receiving SNAP benefits.

Exemptions Removed

Veterans, Homeless, Foster Youth (ages 18-24) no longer exempt

+

What Changed?

Before HR1

These groups were automatically exempt from work requirements:

  • Veterans - All veterans were exempt
  • Homeless individuals - Anyone experiencing homelessness was exempt
  • Foster youth ages 18-24 - Young adults who aged out of foster care were exempt

After HR1

These exemptions were removed. Veterans, homeless individuals, and former foster youth must now meet work requirements unless they qualify for a different exemption (like disability or having a child under 14).

Exemptions That Still Apply

You are still exempt if you:

  • Are pregnant
  • Have a disability or receive disability benefits
  • Are medically certified as unable to work
  • Are caring for an incapacitated household member
  • Are a tribal member (newly exempt under HR1)

When Do These Changes Take Effect?

July 4, 2025

HR1 signed into law - effective immediately

December 1, 2025

Most states began checking work requirements at recertification

June 2026

First possible month for benefit terminations

State Exceptions

These states/territories have waivers delaying enforcement until mid-2026:

California (CA), District of Columbia (DC), Illinois (IL), Nevada (NV), Guam (GU), Virgin Islands (VI)

What Happens Next?

What Counts as Meeting Work Requirements?

You must complete 80 hours per month of one or more of these activities:

  • Work - Paid employment at any wage
  • Workfare - Unpaid work in exchange for benefits
  • Job training - SNAP Employment & Training (E&T) programs
  • Volunteer work - At nonprofit organizations

Note: 80 hours = about 20 hours per week or 4 hours per weekday

What Documentation You'll Need

  • Pay stubs or employer verification letter
  • Volunteer hour logs (signed by supervisor)
  • Training program attendance records
  • Monthly reports submitted to your state SNAP office

What's the Time Limit?

If you don't meet the work requirements, you can only receive SNAP for 3 months in any 36-month period.

After 3 months, your benefits will stop unless you start meeting the work requirements.

When Does This Apply to You?

States check work requirements at your recertification (when you renew your SNAP benefits, typically every 6-12 months).

If you're affected by the new rules, your state will notify you about work requirements at your next recertification.
